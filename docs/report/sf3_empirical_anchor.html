Re-anchoring Stylized Fact 3 (Echo Chamber Depth) — and an Honest Directional Match

Technical report — citation audit, construct correction, and a self-corrected band

Manuscript Manipulating Moderate Hubs in Social Networks Section 3.2 / SF3 Date 2026-06-11 Author T. Takeda

This report documents the audit of SF3 (echo chamber depth), the most problematic of the three stylized facts. It also records a self-correction: an arbitrary lower bound that was initially introduced to make the model "pass" was removed, and SF3 is now reported honestly as a directional (not point) match. Companion to the SF1 and SF2 reports.

1. The problem with the original anchor

The draft validated the model's extreme-agent comfort rate (64.3%) against a band of 55–65% "within-group share," citing Barberá (2015) and Cinelli (2021), with Guess (2021) added in the notebook. All three citations were problematic:

Claim as writtenWhat the source actually says
Guess (2021): partisan diet share ~55–65% Guess is an anti-echo-chamber paper: average diets are moderate/diverse, ~65–70% Dem/Rep overlap, "echo chambers…a reality for relatively few." It argues the opposite of a 55–65% aligned share. MISUSED
Cinelli (2021): "~0.60–0.65 homophily index" Cinelli reports a qualitative correlation between a user's leaning and their neighbors' mean leaning (contour maps); no such index appears. NO SUCH NUMBER
Barberá (2015): within-group share ~55–60% / 80% The paper's thesis is "More Than an Echo Chamber"; it reports relative retweet homophily ratios, not a clean within-group share. NO CLEAN %

Construct & population mismatch

  • The model metric is the comfort rate of extreme-opinion agents — so the comparand should be engaged/partisan-user diet alignment (which is higher than the average user), not an "average user" figure.
  • "Comfort rate (aligned fraction of feed)" is yet another unit, distinct from Cinelli's correlation, Guess's diet overlap, and Barberá's retweet ratios.

2. The honest re-anchor (same construct: aligned fraction of an engaged user's feed)

Bakshy's aligned share = 1 − cross-cutting fraction (liberals 24% cross-cutting → 76% aligned; conservatives 35% → 65%). This is the same construct as the model's comfort rate (the aligned fraction of the feed), so it is the proper numeric anchor. The empirical range for engaged users is therefore ~65–76%.

3. Self-correction: the arbitrary 0.55 floor

An earlier version of this re-anchoring used a band of [0.55, 0.75] so the model's 64.3% would fall inside. The 0.55 had no empirical source — it was a "majority (>50%)" floor — i.e. exactly the unsourced-number problem this whole audit set out to fix. It was removed. The band is now the genuine Bakshy-derived range [0.65, 0.76], and the result is reported honestly even though the model falls just below it.

4. Result — a directional match

Model extreme-agent comfort rate = 64.3% (IQR [57.7%, 70.1%]) sits just below the empirical aligned-share range (~65–76%). SF3 is therefore a directional match, not a point match ≈ DIRECTIONAL: the model reproduces the qualitative signature of a partial echo chamber (majority-aligned, far from total) but a slightly shallower one than observed.

Interpretation (and why this is acceptable). A milder-than-observed echo chamber means the model is a conservative substrate for the backfire effect: it does not pre-bake strong segregation that would inflate the manipulation's downstream impact. The backfire result is therefore not an artifact of an over-tuned echo chamber.

5. Caveats

  • Unit is approximate. The model's comfort rate (aligned fraction within bounded confidence) is conceptually, not identically, the same as Bakshy's aligned-exposure share; hence directional, not point.
  • Population matters. The comparand is engaged/partisan users; average-user figures (Guess) are not applicable and were removed.
  • The literature is genuinely split. Cinelli (strong echo chambers) vs. Guess/Bakshy (more moderate than believed); SF3 takes the engaged-user, partial-chamber reading rather than papering over the split with a single number.
