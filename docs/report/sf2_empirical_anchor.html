Re-anchoring Stylized Fact 2 (Cross-Partisan Repost Fraction) to Conover (2011) & Wojcieszak (2022)

Technical report — citation audit, derivation, and reproduction

Manuscript Manipulating Moderate Hubs in Social Networks Section 3.2 / SF2 Date 2026-06-11 Author T. Takeda

This report documents the audit and re-anchoring of SF2 (cross-partisan repost fraction): why the original anchor was wrong, what the cited literature actually reports, and the new framing (qualitative near-zero lower bound + a single numeric upper reference). Companion to the SF1 report.

1. The problem with the original anchor

The draft validated the model's cross-partisan repost fraction (2.1%) against a band of 2–7%, citing Conover et al. (2011) and Halberstam & Knight (2016). The supporting table (notebook) carried three errors:

Claim as writtenWhat the source actually says
Conover modularity Q ≈ 0.70 (retweet) vs. 0.11 (mention) Conover reports 0.48 and 0.17 — both numbers were wrong. FACTUAL ERROR
Halberstam & Knight (2016): ~3% cross-partisan retweet H&K report 80% / 67% same-ideology following (20–33% cross-following) — an exposure measure, not repost endorsement; no "3%" appears. UNSUPPORTED
"Ng et al. (2022)": ~7% elite cross-retweet The ~7% figure is from Wojcieszak et al. (2022) — wrong author attribution. MISCITED

2. The construct is sound (unlike SF1)

The model measures the fraction of repost edges that cross the opinion midpoint, among partisan agents. A repost is a deliberate endorsement (Conover: "Retweets act as a form of endorsement"). The empirical comparand — the fraction of retweets/shares crossing partisan lines — is the same construct. There is no exposure-vs-production mismatch here (the issue that broke SF1's original anchor). The only problem was the numbers and citations, not the operationalization.

3. Evidence from Conover et al. (2011), full text

Conover et al. analyzed 250k political tweets before the 2010 US midterms, building separate retweet and mention networks. Three quantitative findings establish that cross-partisan reposting is near-zero:

(a) Modularity

"The modularity induced by the communities in the retweet and mention networks have values of 0.48 and 0.17, respectively." (z-scores 11.02 vs 2.06)

(b) Cross-ideological retweet links vs. chance (Table 5)

Observed / expected number of links under random mixing:

retweet network→ Left→ Right
Left1.700.05
Right0.032.32

Cross-partisan retweeting occurs at only 3–5% of its randomly-expected frequency. (For contrast, the mention network is far more mixed: Left→Right 0.68, Right→Left 0.77.)

(c) Cluster purity & explicit statement

Retweet cluster A = 93.4% right; cluster B = 80.1% left (Table 4). The authors conclude:

"...they very rarely share information from across the divide with other members of their community ... political segregation, as manifested in the topology of the retweet network, persists."
Unit caveat. Conover reports observed/expected ratios (0.03–0.05) and cluster purity, not an absolute "% of retweets that cross partisan lines." So Conover anchors the qualitative "near-zero" claim, not a numeric lower bound directly comparable to the model's 2.1%.

4. Upper reference: Wojcieszak et al. (2022)

The only clean, same-construct numeric anchor comes from Wojcieszak et al. (2022), Science Advances (1.5M users, 4 years):

  • In-group vs. out-group political-elite following: 90% / 10%.
  • Sharing from in-group elites 13× more often than from out-group elites.
  • ⇒ out-group share fraction ≈ 1/(1+13) = ~7% of partisan shares cross the divide.

Sharing is endorsement, so this is the correct construct for an upper reference.

5. Result

Model cross-partisan repost fraction = 2.1% (IQR [0.6%, 4.3%]) sits in the near-zero regime, below the ~7% upper reference and consistent with Conover's "very rarely share across the divide." SF2 PASS

7. Caveats

  • Lower bound is qualitative. Conover gives obs/expected ratios and purity, not an absolute cross fraction; we therefore claim "near-zero," not a numeric floor.
  • Upper reference is a single study (Wojcieszak 2022), on elite-content sharing — a related but not identical slice of repost behavior.
  • Era/platform differences. Conover (2010 midterms) and Wojcieszak (2016–2019) span different periods and samples; both nonetheless agree the cross-partisan endorsement rate is small.
  • Sasahara et al. (2021) excluded as an anchor. It is itself an ABM (influence + unfollowing) validated against Conover-type data, so it is a modeling comparand, not independent empirical ground truth; cited elsewhere for the unfollowing mechanism.
