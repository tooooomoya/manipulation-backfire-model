1 · Graph Representation and Edge Semantics

All simulated networks are stored as directed graphs (networkx.DiGraph). An edge $i \to j$ means agent $i$ follows agent $j$: $i$ is exposed to $j$'s posts and is influenced by $j$'s opinion. The distinction from an undirected graph is substantive, not merely notational.

ConceptDirected interpretation
In-degree of $j$Number of agents who follow $j$ — follower count, the primary influence carrier
Out-degree of $i$Number of agents $i$ follows — following count
Reciprocal edge $i \leftrightarrow j$Mutual follow; both agents directly influence each other
Directed triangleOrdered triple $(u,v,w)$ such that at least two of the three possible directed paths exist

Throughout this document, $n = |V|$ is the number of nodes, $m = |E|$ is the number of directed edges, $\mathbf{A}$ is the directed adjacency matrix ($A_{ij} = 1$ iff $i \to j$ exists), and $k_i^{\mathrm{in}}, k_i^{\mathrm{out}}$ denote in-degree and out-degree of node $i$, respectively.

3 · Network Density

Fully directed

Density measures the fraction of all possible directed edges that actually exist.

$$d = \frac{m}{n(n-1)}$$

The denominator $n(n-1)$ (not $\tfrac{n(n-1)}{2}$) reflects that the maximum possible number of directed edges between $n$ nodes is $n(n-1)$, since $i \to j$ and $j \to i$ are distinct. In a complete directed graph $d = 1$; in an empty graph $d = 0$.

NetworkX call

nx.density(G)   # G : nx.DiGraph

NetworkX's density function is direction-aware: it checks G.is_directed() and applies the $n(n-1)$ denominator for directed graphs.

Interpretive note. In preferential-attachment networks density is typically very low ($d \ll 0.01$) and decreasing as $n$ grows. A rise in density over simulation steps indicates accelerating edge formation, which in this model signals reinforcement of existing follower relationships rather than network sparsification.

4 · Clustering Coefficient — Fagiolo (2007)

Fully directed

The classical Watts–Strogatz clustering coefficient is defined for undirected graphs. Applying it to a directed graph by ignoring edge direction collapses the distinction between a node's follower and following circles, producing a measure that is insensitive to the asymmetric information-flow structure central to this model.

4.1 Node-Level Formula

The directed clustering coefficient due to Fagiolo (2007) — which NetworkX implements for DiGraph — counts directed triangles weighted by both their presence and their orientation:

$$c_i = \frac{T(i)}{2\!\left[k_i^{\mathrm{tot}}\!\left(k_i^{\mathrm{tot}}-1\right) - 2k_i^{\leftrightarrow}\right]}$$

where:

  • $k_i^{\mathrm{tot}} = k_i^{\mathrm{in}} + k_i^{\mathrm{out}}$ — total degree (in + out)
  • $k_i^{\leftrightarrow}$ — reciprocal (bilateral) degree: number of neighbors $j$ such that both $i \to j$ and $j \to i$ exist
  • $T(i)$ — number of directed triangles through $i$, counting all distinct orientations in which $i$ participates (cycles, fan-in, fan-out, middleman).

The denominator subtracts $2k_i^{\leftrightarrow}$ because reciprocal edges would otherwise be counted twice in the $k_i^{\mathrm{tot}}(k_i^{\mathrm{tot}}-1)$ maximum, inflating the denominator unfairly for hubs with many mutual follows. When $k_i^{\mathrm{tot}} < 2$ (isolated or degree-1 nodes), $c_i = 0$ by convention.

4.2 Network-Level Average

$$\bar{C} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i \in V} c_i$$

NetworkX calls

nx.average_clustering(G)    # network-level mean
nx.clustering(G, node_id)   # per-node, used in structural analysis

Both calls dispatch to the Fagiolo formula when G is a DiGraph.

Limitation. The Fagiolo coefficient conflates structurally different triangle types (cycle, fan-in, fan-out) into a single scalar. In opinion-dynamics contexts, cycle triangles ($i \to j \to k \to i$) and fan-in triangles ($j \to i$, $k \to i$, $j \to k$) carry very different implications for information reinforcement. Decomposing $c_i$ by motif type (Zhang & Lun 2009) would yield sharper structural predictors of echo-chamber formation, but is not currently computed.

5 · Power-Law Exponent γ — Clauset, Shalizi & Newman (2009)

Fully directed

A scale-free degree distribution satisfies $P(k) \sim k^{-\gamma}$. For directed social networks, in-degree (follower count) is the theoretically motivated choice: hub influence is determined by how many agents receive from them, not by how many they follow. Out-degree is set partly by agent strategy and platform constraints; in-degree reflects emergent social selection.

5.1 MLE Estimator

For a discrete power law with lower bound $k_{\min}$, the maximum-likelihood estimator (Clauset et al. 2009, eq. 3.3) is:

$$\hat{\gamma} = 1 + n_{\mathrm{tail}} \left[\,\sum_{i=1}^{n_{\mathrm{tail}}} \ln\frac{k_i}{k_{\min} - \tfrac{1}{2}}\right]^{-1}$$

where $n_{\mathrm{tail}}$ is the number of nodes with $k^{\mathrm{in}} \geq k_{\min}$. The $-\tfrac{1}{2}$ correction is the standard discrete continuity adjustment.

5.2 $k_{\min}$ Selection

$k_{\min}$ is not fixed a priori; it is chosen to minimize the Kolmogorov–Smirnov (KS) distance between the empirical survival function and the Pareto approximation used as a continuous surrogate:

$$\hat{k}_{\min} = \operatorname*{arg\,min}_{k_{\min}} \sup_k \left|F_{\mathrm{emp}}(k) - \left(\frac{k}{k_{\min}}\right)^{-(\hat{\gamma}-1)}\right|$$

The search is conducted over unique observed in-degree values. To avoid tail noise dominating the fit, the search terminates at the first $k_{\min}$ for which fewer than 5 nodes remain in the tail.

Implementation

degrees = np.array([d for _, d in G.in_degree()], dtype=float)  # in-degree only
gamma_hat, k_min = _mle_powerlaw(degrees)
Validity check. The MLE produces a point estimate regardless of whether the true distribution is actually a power law. A rigorous test (e.g., the likelihood-ratio test against log-normal or exponential alternatives; Clauset et al. 2009, §4) is not computed here. The $\gamma$ value should be interpreted as a descriptive summary of tail heaviness, not as confirmation of scale-freeness.

6 · Modularity Q — Two-Step Directed Procedure

Hybrid (partition undirected / score directed)

Modularity $Q$ quantifies the degree to which a network decomposes into densely connected communities, relative to a random null graph with the same degree sequence. High modularity implies strong echo-chamber structure; values near zero indicate no detectable community organisation.

6.1 Why Two Steps Are Necessary

The standard Louvain algorithm (Blondel et al. 2008) optimises the undirected modularity objective and is not defined for directed graphs. At the same time, simply converting the directed graph to undirected before scoring would apply the wrong null model — one based on total degree rather than on the asymmetric generating process. The two-step procedure resolves this tension:

  1. Partition discovery (undirected projection). The directed graph is projected to an undirected graph $G_{\mathrm{und}}$ via G.to_undirected(), which places an undirected edge $\{i,j\}$ whenever $i \to j$ or $j \to i$ (or both) exists. Louvain community detection runs on $G_{\mathrm{und}}$. This step identifies cohesive groups based on mutual connectivity, which is appropriate for echo-chamber analysis: strong echo chambers are characterised by dense bidirectional interaction within the group.
  2. Partition scoring (directed graph). The community assignment $\{c_1, \ldots, c_k\}$ discovered in step 1 is evaluated against the original directed graph $G$ using the Leicht–Newman directed modularity formula. This scores how well the partition explains the directed edge structure relative to the directed configuration-model null.

6.2 Directed Modularity Formula (Leicht & Newman 2008)

$$Q = \frac{1}{m}\sum_{c}\left[L_c - \frac{k_c^{\mathrm{out}}\, k_c^{\mathrm{in}}}{m}\right]$$

where for each community $c$:

  • $m = |E|$ — total number of directed edges
  • $L_c = \sum_{i,j \in c} A_{ij}$ — number of directed edges internal to community $c$
  • $k_c^{\mathrm{out}} = \sum_{i \in c} k_i^{\mathrm{out}}$ — total out-degree of community $c$
  • $k_c^{\mathrm{in}} = \sum_{i \in c} k_i^{\mathrm{in}} $ — total in-degree of community $c$

The null model term $k_c^{\mathrm{out}} k_c^{\mathrm{in}} / m^2$ is the expected fraction of all directed edges falling within community $c$ if edges were placed independently with probability proportional to $k_i^{\mathrm{out}} \cdot k_j^{\mathrm{in}} / m$ — the directed configuration model. This is the correct null for a preferential-attachment follower network, where high-in-degree nodes attract more incoming edges and high-out-degree nodes send more.

6.3 Contrast with Undirected Formula

For comparison, the undirected Newman–Girvan formula (which would result from evaluating on G_und) is:

$$Q_{\mathrm{undir}} = \frac{1}{2m_{\mathrm{u}}}\sum_c\left[L_c^{\mathrm{u}} - \frac{k_c^2}{2m_{\mathrm{u}}}\right]$$

where $m_{\mathrm{u}}$ is the undirected edge count and $k_c = \sum_{i \in c} k_i$ (total degree, not distinguished by direction). Applying this formula to a directed follower network would treat a node's followers and followees symmetrically, ignoring the asymmetric influence mechanism that motivates the model.

NetworkX calls

G_und = G.to_undirected()

# Step 1: community detection on undirected projection
try:
    communities = nx.community.louvain_communities(G_und, seed=42)
except AttributeError:                           # NetworkX < 2.7 fallback
    communities = list(nx.community.greedy_modularity_communities(G_und))

# Step 2: score on original directed graph — NetworkX branches on G.is_directed()
Q = nx.community.modularity(G, communities)
NetworkX implementation detail. nx.community.modularity(G, communities) inspects G.is_directed() internally. For directed graphs it computes $k_c^{\mathrm{out}} k_c^{\mathrm{in}}$ as the null-model term; for undirected graphs it uses $k_c^2$. Passing the original DiGraph (not the undirected projection) to this call is therefore sufficient to activate the directed formula automatically.
Caveat on partition origin. Because the community partition is derived from the undirected projection, it maximises an objective that does not account for edge direction. A high-$Q$ community in the directed sense might contain structural asymmetries (e.g., a star topology where all edges point inward) that would not constitute a "community" in opinion-dynamics terms. Interpreting $Q$ as an echo-chamber proxy therefore assumes that mutual-follow density is a valid surrogate for bidirectional opinion reinforcement — an assumption that is standard in CSS but not formally guaranteed.

7 · In-Degree

Fully directed
$$k_i^{\mathrm{in}} = \sum_{j \in V} A_{ji}$$

In-degree counts the number of agents who follow node $i$ — its follower count. It is the primary influence measure in the model: the admin selects intervention targets from the pool of moderate-opinion nodes with follower count above a minimum threshold, so $k_i^{\mathrm{in}}$ directly governs target eligibility and expected reach.

NetworkX call

G.in_degree(node_id)   # returns int for a single node

Out-degree ($k_i^{\mathrm{out}}$) is not included as a structural predictor because in this model agents follow based on opinion proximity, so out-degree reflects filtering behaviour rather than social influence.

8 · Betweenness Centrality

Fully directed

Betweenness centrality measures the fraction of all directed shortest paths that pass through a node, making it a proxy for brokerage or gatekeeping power.

$$B(v) = \frac{1}{(n-1)(n-2)}\sum_{\substack{s,t \in V \\ s \neq v \neq t}} \frac{\sigma(s,t \mid v)}{\sigma(s,t)}$$

where:

  • $\sigma(s,t)$ — number of directed shortest paths from $s$ to $t$
  • $\sigma(s,t \mid v)$ — number of those paths that pass through $v$
  • The normalisation constant $(n-1)(n-2)$ is the maximum number of ordered node pairs $(s, t)$ that exclude $v$ in a directed graph (cf. $\tfrac{(n-1)(n-2)}{2}$ in the undirected case)

The direction matters: a node that sits on many $s \to t$ paths but few $t \to s$ paths has high directed betweenness because it gates the flow of influence in one direction. An undirected betweenness computation would conflate these asymmetric brokerage roles.

NetworkX call

bc_all = nx.betweenness_centrality(G, normalized=True)  # G : DiGraph

NetworkX uses Brandes' algorithm (2001) adapted for directed graphs: a forward BFS from each source computes $\sigma(s, \cdot)$; a backward accumulation pass distributes the dependency scores. Complexity is $O(nm)$ for unweighted graphs. With $n \approx 10^3$–$10^4$ and sparse networks, this is the computationally dominant step in the structural analysis pipeline.

9 · PageRank

Fully directed

PageRank assigns centrality scores by simulating a random walk with teleportation. A walker at node $i$ follows a uniformly random out-edge with probability $\alpha$ (damping factor) and jumps to a uniformly random node with probability $1-\alpha$.

$$\mathrm{PR}(i) = \frac{1-\alpha}{n} + \alpha \sum_{j:\, j\to i} \frac{\mathrm{PR}(j)}{k_j^{\mathrm{out}}}$$

A node has high PageRank if many high-PageRank nodes follow it and those followers are otherwise selective (low out-degree). This combines in-degree with qualitative weighting — a single follower who follows very few agents contributes more to $\mathrm{PR}$ than ten followers who follow everyone.

NetworkX call

pr_all = nx.pagerank(G, alpha=0.85, max_iter=200)

The damping factor $\alpha = 0.85$ is the canonical value from the original Brin & Page (1998) paper and is standard in CSS network analysis. The maximum iteration count is raised from the NetworkX default (100) to 200 to improve convergence on large or poorly-conditioned graphs.

Relation to in-degree. In a directed configuration model (edges drawn independently proportional to degree sequence), PageRank converges to $\mathrm{PR}(i) \propto k_i^{\mathrm{in}}$ as $\alpha \to 1$. In structured networks with hubs, PageRank and in-degree diverge: PageRank rewards being followed by influential agents, while in-degree treats all followers equally. The Spearman correlation between the two metrics is therefore informative about the homophily structure of the follower network.

10 · Eigenvector Centrality — Left Eigenvector

Fully directed

Eigenvector centrality generalises degree centrality by weighting each in-neighbor's contribution by that neighbor's own centrality, creating a recursive definition of prestige.

10.1 Formula and Convention

For directed graphs, two eigenvector variants exist. This implementation uses the left eigenvector of the adjacency matrix $\mathbf{A}$, the solution $\mathbf{x}$ to:

$$\lambda\, \mathbf{x}^{\top} = \mathbf{x}^{\top} \mathbf{A} \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \lambda\, x_i = \sum_{j \to i} x_j$$

Node $i$'s score equals (up to the eigenvalue $\lambda$) the sum of centrality scores of nodes that follow $i$ (in-neighbors/predecessors). This is an authority/prestige score: a node is central if it is followed by other central nodes.

Terminology note. In the HITS framework (Kleinberg 1999), this quantity is called the authority score, not the hub score. The hub score would be the right eigenvector ($\mathbf{Ax} = \lambda\mathbf{x}$, summing over out-neighbors). The notebook description "left eigenvector (hub score)" conflates these; the computation is authority-based. The authority score is the correct choice for characterising influence in a follower network, because it rewards being selected as a followee by prestigious agents.

10.2 Convergence and Fallback

Power iteration is used with max_iter=1000 and tol=1e-6. Convergence is not guaranteed when $G$ is not strongly connected (the Perron–Frobenius theorem applies to strongly connected graphs; for weakly connected or disconnected directed graphs, the dominant eigenvector may not be unique, or some entries may be zero). In such cases nx.PowerIterationFailedConvergence is caught and the metric is recorded as NaN rather than propagating an error.

NetworkX call

try:
    ec_all = nx.eigenvector_centrality(G, max_iter=1000, tol=1e-6)
    eigenvector = ec_all.get(target_id, np.nan)
except nx.PowerIterationFailedConvergence:
    eigenvector = np.nan

Compared with PageRank, eigenvector centrality has no teleportation term and no out-degree normalisation. It can therefore be more sensitive to structural asymmetries (e.g., a node followed by a single extremely central agent may outscore a node with many low-centrality followers).
