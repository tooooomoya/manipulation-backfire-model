Structural Backfire Findings

Branch: appnet  |  Date: 2026-05-01  |  Author: Tomoya Takeda

Overview

This session analyses the relationship between the structural position of the manipulation target and the magnitude of the backfire effect, using the 196-run dataset (98 seeds × 2 directions). The central finding is a two-regime structure: direction-dependent backfire in the majority of seeds, and direction-invariant (robust) backfire in a small subset of seeds characterised by very high target in-degree. The session also adds a follower opinion homophily module to the analysis notebook and evaluates a network-scaling strategy for future experiments.

Interpretation

  • Effect sizes are small: ρ ≈ 0.14–0.19 corresponds to roughly 3% explained variance. The pooled analysis captures a real signal but not a dominant one.
  • In-degree, PageRank, and eigenvector centrality are near-perfectly collinear (in-degree ∼ PageRank: ρ = 0.914). All three measure the same underlying quantity — follower reach. Reporting them as independent predictors overstates the evidence; the operative claim is simply: follower count weakly but reliably predicts backfire magnitude.
  • Betweenness null is informative: bridge/broker position does not predict backfire. Backfire is a reach phenomenon, not a brokerage phenomenon. Nodes sitting between opinion clusters face cancelling pressures from both sides.
  • Negative clustering trend (n.s.): targets embedded in dense local triangles tend toward slightly less backfire, consistent with echo-chamber shielding — their followers are internally cohesive and less reactive to the manipulation signal from outside. The marginal significance warrants caution.

2. Direction Asymmetry: Paired Within-Seed Analysis

Each seed generates two runs (dir = ±1.0) with an identical network and target at t = 20,000 (confirmed: 0 target-ID mismatches across 98 seeds). Any observed asymmetry between directions is therefore entirely attributable to post-intervention dynamics, not to network structure differences.

Only 16 of 98 seed pairs agree on the sign of the backfire effect. The dominant pattern (82 pairs, 84%) is that the two directions disagree — backfire occurs in one direction and not the other. A paired t-test of the per-seed backfire difference (dir=−1 minus dir=+1) returns t = 0.94, p = 0.35, indicating no systematic mean offset, but the 45:37 split shows that dir=−1 is slightly more likely to produce backfire (60% positive vs 52% for dir=+1).

Mechanistic note: The 45:37 asymmetry implies a mild positive bias in the opinion distribution at t = 20,000 — the network leans slightly right of centre on average. Pushing the target toward −1 therefore goes against the structural tide, creating stronger counter-mobilisation. The source is the stochastic seed assignment: the top-2 follower nodes receive intrinsic opinions {−1, +1} via a shuffled assignment; because top-1 has more followers than top-2, whichever opinion camp it joins dominates. This is an irreducible feature of the initialisation design and averages out across seeds, but produces cross-seed structural variation in network polarity.

Implication for the pooled Spearman analysis

The direction split reveals that the pooled ρ conflates two distinct regimes. For dir=+1 the in-degree → backfire correlation is ρ = +0.207 (p = 0.040); for dir=−1 it is ρ = +0.169 (p = 0.097, n.s.). The structural effect is cleaner in the direction where the network already exerts mild resistance (dir=+1 into the positive-biased network). The correct framing is an interaction between target reach and manipulation direction relative to network polarity, not a simple main effect.

follower_dir_alignment directly operationalises the counter-mobilisation mechanism: if the target's followers are predominantly aligned with the manipulation direction, they will collectively notice the shift in the target's posting and react against the direction it was nudged in. It is the most theoretically motivated single predictor and should be checked for a stronger correlation with backfire than raw in-degree once the notebook is re-run on fresh data.

Other homophily measures considered: community-based homophily (GEXF includes a community attribute) was inspected and found to carry value −1 for all nodes in examined runs, indicating community detection was not executed at output time. It was excluded. Bounded-confidence and activity-based homophily were also considered but are model parameters rather than emergent structural properties.

4. Direction-Invariant (Robust) Backfire: The 14 Double-Backfire Seeds

The odds ratio peaks near in-degree ≈ 300, suggesting an empirically motivated structural threshold. Above this level, the target's follower pool is large enough to contain substantial numbers of both opinion camps simultaneously, guaranteeing a counter-mobilisation response in either manipulation direction.

Within-group: magnitude scales with reach

Within the 14 double-backfire seeds, in-degree correlates strongly with mean backfire magnitude (average over both directions): Spearman ρ = 0.837, p = 0.0002. PageRank gives ρ = 0.785, p = 0.0009. The relationship is not merely binary (does/doesn't backfire) but continuous: more followers → larger counter-mobilisation in both directions.

Mechanistic account

A moderate target (opinion ≈ 0) with many followers has accumulated that following from both opinion camps: both positive- and negative-leaning agents were within its bounded-confidence radius and chose to follow it. When the manipulation nudges the target toward +1 by 0.01/step, the target's posts shift into bins 3–4. Its negative-leaning followers — who previously received near-neutral content — now encounter dissonant posts and mobilise: they increase posting probability and amplify their existing stances. The symmetric effect occurs under the −1 direction.

At lower in-degree, the follower base is too small or too ideologically homogeneous to mount a detectable response on the weaker side. The effect is asymmetric in direction, reducing to the single-direction backfire pattern seen in the majority of seeds.

CSS connection: This maps to the structural embeddedness → reactance resistance thesis. The finding inverts the echo-chamber-as-protection frame (Guess & Lyons 2020; Bail et al. 2018): it is the absence of an echo chamber — a large, ideologically diverse follower base — that makes a target manipulation-resistant. The most reachable moderates are the most robust to influence campaigns.

5. Network Scaling Strategy for Future Experiments

The current setup (N = 1000, DMS m = 3, A = 1, γ = 2.33) produces approximately 0.5 expected nodes with in-degree ≥ 300 in the static initial topology. The observed count is higher owing to 20,000 steps of follow dynamics, but the double-backfire regime is reached in only 14% of seeds. Two levers are available to increase this fraction.

Why "decrease scale-freeness" is the wrong framing

In the DMS model, γ = 2 + A/m. Increasing A (reducing scale-freeness, raising γ) destroys large hubs:

The metric dilution caveat

The backfire metric is a global post-share fraction. When N scales from 1000 to 5000 with everything else proportional, the absolute counter-mobilisation grows ~5× but so does the denominator (total posts). The measured proportion therefore stays roughly constant. The value of scaling N is not to amplify each individual measurement but to increase the number of observations in the high-degree regime — more seeds land near or above the structural threshold.

If per-measurement signal quality is the bottleneck, the cleaner alternative is a local backfire metric: measure opinion change in the target's 2-hop neighborhood rather than in global post shares. This sharpens the signal without requiring a larger N and is more mechanistically honest about where the effect manifests.

Key risk: moderate pool collapse at large m

Higher m densifies the initial graph and accelerates polarisation through the two extreme seeds. The moderate target pool (agents with |opinion| < 0.2 at t = 20,000) may shrink, reducing candidate diversity across seeds. A pilot run at N = 3000, m = 5 should check the logged "Moderate-influencer pool size" before committing to a full experiment. If the pool drops significantly, widen the moderation criterion from |opinion| < 0.2 to |opinion| < 0.3.

Recommendation

Pilot: N = 3000, m = 5, 20–30 seeds. This is ~3× the compute cost of the current setup, produces ~4× more high-degree moderate targets, and validates pool stability before scaling to N = 5000, m = 8.

Key Findings Summary

  1. Follower count (in-degree) is the operative structural predictor of backfire, not betweenness or clustering. PageRank and eigenvector centrality are collinear proxies for the same quantity (ρ = 0.914 with in-degree); they should not be reported as independent predictors.
  2. Backfire is predominantly direction-dependent: 84% of same-seed pairs disagree on the sign of the backfire effect. Direction reverses the outcome more than structure determines it in the pooled analysis.
  3. The 14 double-backfire seeds are the theoretically clean signal: direction-invariant, significantly higher in-degree (median 344 vs 110, MWU p = 0.008, rank-biserial r = −0.45), and strongly correlated within-group (in-degree ∼ mean backfire magnitude: ρ = 0.837, p = 0.0002).
  4. An empirical structural threshold exists near in-degree ≈ 300: odds ratio for robust backfire peaks at ~10× at this threshold.
  5. Mechanism: very high-degree moderate targets attract followers from both opinion camps. Manipulation in either direction mobilises the opposing camp among those followers. Below the threshold, the follower base is too small or too homogeneous for bidirectional reactance.
  6. Next experiment: N = 3000, m = 5 pilot to validate the structural threshold under a larger-hub regime, while keeping MAX_FOLLOW_CAPACITY fixed and checking for moderate pool stability.
