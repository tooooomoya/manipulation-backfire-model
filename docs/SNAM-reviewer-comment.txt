Reviewer 1
This study only extending parameters and topologies .The model is oversimplified by ignoring algorithmic recommendation and real user dynamics. The small-scale simulation with only 2 manipulated hubs and no empirical validation undermines credibility.

Reviewer 2
The paper “Manipulating Moderate Hubs in Social Networks: Boundary Conditions and Robustness of the Backfire Effect” presents a rigorous and timely investigation into how influence operations can produce unintended consequences in online social networks. Specifically, it examines the “backfire effect,” a counterintuitive phenomenon in which attempts to shift moderately positioned but highly connected users (hubs) toward extreme opinions fail to steer collective opinion and instead intensify polarization. Building on prior preliminary work, the authors aim to generalize this effect and identify the structural and behavioral conditions under which it emerges.

The study develops a detailed agent-based model (ABM) grounded in established theories of opinion dynamics, including bounded confidence and social influence. A notable strength of the model is its incorporation of both attraction and repulsion mechanisms, allowing agents not only to converge toward similar opinions but also to actively distance themselves from dissimilar ones. This is a meaningful extension beyond classical models, as it better reflects real-world online interactions where disagreement often leads to disengagement or antagonism. The model further captures key social media behaviors such as posting, reposting, following, and unfollowing, along with adaptive changes in user activity and openness to opposing views. These features contribute to a relatively rich and dynamic representation of online social systems.

Methodologically, the paper is strong. The authors conduct a comprehensive sensitivity analysis across key behavioral parameters, particularly agent stubbornness and conformity thresholds, and evaluate outcomes across multiple network topologies, including scale-free networks (CNNR and Barabási–Albert) and random networks (Erdős–Rényi). Their findings demonstrate that the backfire effect is robust across a wide range of parameter settings in scale-free networks but largely absent in random networks. Moreover, the results highlight the critical role of agent stubbornness: higher resistance to opinion change amplifies the effect, while lower stubbornness diminishes it. The use of repeated simulations, confidence intervals, and statistical testing adds credibility and rigor to the analysis.

One of the paper’s key contributions is the identification of boundary conditions that explain when and why the backfire effect occurs. The authors convincingly argue that the scale-free structure of social networks, characterized by highly connected hub nodes, is essential to the phenomenon. When moderate hubs are pushed to extreme positions, they lose connections with followers holding opposing views, which fragments the network and accelerates the formation of echo chambers. This structural disconnection, rather than simple opinion contagion, appears to drive the observed polarization. The paper thus provides an important theoretical insight: influence operations targeting central actors may destabilize network connectivity in ways that undermine their intended goals.

Despite these strengths, the paper has several limitations that temper its contributions. The most significant concern is the lack of empirical validation. While the simulation framework is carefully constructed and grounded in prior literature, the findings remain theoretical. There is no direct comparison with real-world social media data, making it difficult to assess how well the model captures actual user behavior or platform dynamics. Incorporating empirical validation—either through observational data or controlled experiments—would substantially strengthen the claims and enhance the study’s practical relevance.

The modeling assumptions, although necessary for tractability, also limit realism. The manipulation scenario is simplified to forcing two hub nodes to adopt extreme opinions, which does not fully capture the complexity of real-world influence operations that may involve coordinated campaigns, multiple actors, or gradual opinion shifts. Additionally, agents are relatively homogeneous in their behavioral rules, and opinions are represented along a single continuous dimension. This abstraction overlooks the multidimensional, identity-driven, and context-dependent nature of real beliefs and attitudes. The absence of platform-level mechanisms, such as algorithmic recommendation systems and content ranking, is another important omission, given their well-documented role in shaping information exposure and polarization.

Another limitation lies in the interpretation of the underlying mechanism. While the authors attribute the backfire effect to unfollowing behavior and the resulting fragmentation of network ties, this causal pathway is not directly measured or isolated in the analysis. The explanation is plausible and consistent with prior research, but additional evidence—such as explicit tracking of network connectivity changes or clustering dynamics—would make the argument more convincing.

In terms of presentation, the paper is technically solid but occasionally dense, particularly in its formalization of the model and explanation of metrics. While appropriate for a specialist audience, clearer intuitive explanations and more accessible descriptions of key results would broaden its appeal to interdisciplinary readers.

Overall, this paper makes a meaningful contribution to computational social science by advancing our understanding of how influence strategies interact with network structure and individual behavior. It highlights the possibility that attempts to manipulate influential users may produce unintended and counterproductive outcomes, especially in scale-free networks where hubs play a critical role in maintaining connectivity. The identification of structural vulnerability and the emphasis on the interplay between network topology and behavioral parameters are particularly valuable.

In conclusion, the study is theoretically robust and methodologically rigorous, offering important insights into opinion dynamics and influence operations. However, its impact would be significantly enhanced by empirical validation, greater realism in the modeling framework, and more direct analysis of the mechanisms driving the observed effects. With these improvements, the work has strong potential to become an influential contribution to the literature on social networks, polarization, and information manipulation.
